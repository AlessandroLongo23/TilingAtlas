           A canonical form for integer symbols of periodic tilings
                            by regular polygons
                                           TilingAtlas working note*

                                                   July 9, 2026



                                                     Abstract
            Soto Sánchez, Weyrich, Medeiros e Sá and de Figueiredo encode an edge-to-edge periodic
        tiling of the plane by unit-edge regular polygons as a (2 + n) × 4 integer matrix over Z[ω],
        ω = e2πi/12 : two rows span a period lattice, the remaining n rows list one vertex per translation
        class. One tiling has infinitely many symbols. We define a computable canonical form N and
        prove it sound and canonical for the equivalence V ′ = gV + c with g in the dihedral group D12
        and c ∈ Z[ω]. The lattice-basis, seed-representative and seed-order ambiguities are eliminated
        by construction (Hermite normal form, canonical coset representatives, row sorting); the 24n-
        fold frame ambiguity (orientation and origin) is cut to the stabilizer of an intrinsic invariant,
        the multiset of vertex stars, and the residual minimization is proved to compare byte-identical
        candidates whenever its ties come from symmetries of the tiling. Along the way we exhibit
        a defect in the naive symbol equivalence: the four commonly listed ambiguity sources do not
        generate equivalence of vertex sets, because a tiling can be re-encoded over any finite-index
        sublattice of its period lattice; a correct canonical form must therefore recompute the maximal
        translation lattice, and ours does. We give complexity bounds, run the form on the required
        worked instances, and report an adversarial verification campaign in which no counterexample
        to canonicity was found.


1       Setting
Let ω = e2πi/12 , with minimal polynomial Φ12 (x) = x4 − x2 + 1, so ω 4 = ω 2 − 1. The ring Z[ω] is a
free Z-module with basis (1, ω, ω 2 , ω 3 ); we identify p ∈ Z[ω] with its coordinate row vector in Z4 .
Multiplication by ω and complex conjugation are Z-linear maps preserving Z[ω]. Following [1],
an edge-to-edge periodic tiling by unit-edge regular polygons, normalized so that one vertex is 0
and all edges point along ω 0 , . . . , ω 11 (this excludes exactly the octagon-bearing truncated square
tiling, whose edges lie off the 30◦ grid), is recorded by its vertex set V ⊂ Z[ω]: choosing a rank-2
period lattice Λ = Zt1 + Zt2 with t1 , t2 R-linearly independent in C and V + Λ = V , the set V is
a finite union of cosets

where G = {ρj : z ↦→ ω j z} ∪ {µj : z ↦→ ω j z̄} is the dihedral group D12 of order 24, acting by integer
matrices on Z4 . We write M ∼ M ′ when the vertex sets of two symbols satisfy (1). A canonical

   The classical inventory of symbol ambiguities lists four sources: (1) the lattice basis, U ∈
GL2 (Z); (2) each seed representative, mod Λ; (3) the seed order; (4) the frame, i.e. origin (n
choices) and orientation (g ∈ G, 24 choices). Source 4 is the substantial one; a brute-force baseline
canonicalizes by minimizing over all 24n frames. Section 3 shows this list is incomplete as a
generating set for (1).


2    Preliminaries
Lemma 2.1 (Hermite normal form, rank 2 in Z4 ). Every rank-2 sublattice Λ ⊂ Z4 has a unique
basis (h1 , h2 ) such that, writing ci for the position of the first nonzero entry of hi and pi = hi [ci ]:
c1 < c2 , p1 , p2 > 0, and 0 ≤ h1 [c2 ] < p2 . Uniqueness includes the entries outside the pivot columns
c1 , c2 .

Proof. Let c1 be the first coordinate not identically zero on Λ and p1 > 0 the generator of the
image of the projection πc1 : Λ → Z. The kernel Λ2 = {v ∈ Λ : v[c1 ] = 0} satisfies Λ/Λ2 ∼   = Z, so it
has rank 1 and a unique generator h2 whose first nonzero entry p2 := h2 [c2 ] is positive; its pivot c2
exceeds c1 because all of Λ vanishes on coordinates before c1 . Pick u ∈ Λ with u[c1 ] = p1 and set
h1 = u − ⌊u[c2 ]/p2 ⌋h2 ; any two candidates differ by an element of Λ2 = Zh2 whose c2 -entry lies in
(−p2 , p2 ), hence by 0. Both h1 and h2 are thereby pinned as vectors, free columns included. See
also [3, §2.4] for the general theory.

Definition 2.2 (canonical coset representative). For Λ with HNF basis (h1 , h2 ) as above and
v ∈ Z4 , let repΛ (v) be the unique element r ∈ v + Λ with r[c1 ] ∈ [0, p1 ) and r[c2 ] ∈ [0, p2 ),
computed by two floor divisions (reduce by h1 , then by h2 ; the second step does not disturb
coordinate c1 since h2 [c1 ] = 0).

    Uniqueness: a difference ah1 +bh2 with both pivot coordinates in the open box forces |a|p1 < p1 ,
so a = 0, then b = 0. In particular repΛ depends only on the coset and on Λ itself, not on any
basis, and repΛ (0) = 0.

Lemma 2.3 (direction covariance). For g ∈ G define σg ∈ Sym(Z/12) by σρj (k) = j + k and
σµj (k) = j − k. Then g(v + ω k ) = g(v) + ω σg (k) for all v ∈ Z[ω], and g ↦→ σg is a group
homomorphism.

Proof. g is Z-linear and g(ω k ) equals ω j+k for g = ρj and ω j ω k = ω j−k for g = µj . The homomor-
phism property follows from (g1 g2 )(ω k ) = g1 (ω σg2 (k) ) = ω σg1 σg2 (k) .


3    Normalizing the lattice, and a defect in the four-source list
For a vertex set V let Λmax (V ) = {t ∈ Z[ω] : t + V = V }, the full translation lattice.



                                                    2
Lemma 3.1 (Stage A). Let (Λ, S) encode V with S = {s1 , . . . , sn } ⊂ Z[ω]/Λ, and let T = {τ ∈
Z[ω]/Λ : S + τ = S}. Then:
 (a) T is a subgroup of Z[ω]/Λ and Λmax (V ) is its full preimage in Z[ω]; in particular Λ ⊆
     Λmax (V ).
 (b) Every τ ∈ T occurs among the n candidates sj − s1 , j = 1, . . . , n.
 (c) For a candidate τ , the inclusion S + τ ⊆ S already implies S + τ = S.
 (d) Every τ ∈ T has finite order in Z[ω]/Λ; hence the lattice generated by Λ and lifts of T has
     rank 2 and equals Λmax (V ).
 (e) Λmax (hV + c) = h Λmax (V ) for all h ∈ G, c ∈ Z[ω].
Proof. (a) t + V = V iff (S + t) + Λ = S + Λ iff S + (t mod Λ) = S. (b) τ + s1 ∈ S, so τ ≡ sj − s1
for some j. (c) Adding τ is injective on Z[ω]/Λ and S is finite, so an injection of S into itself is
a bijection. (d) The orbit {s1 + mτ }m≥0 lies in the finite set S, so mτ = m′ τ for some m > m′
and (m − m′ )τ = 0; thus Λmax ⊗ Q = Λ ⊗ Q has rank 2, and Λmax is generated by Λ together
with the finitely many elements of T , all of which appear in (b). (e) t + hV + c = hV + c iff
h−1 t + V = V .

    Stage A of our algorithm computes Λmax by Lemma 3.1: test the n candidates, stack the valid
ones on Λ, take the HNF (Lemma 2.1), reduce all seeds by repΛmax and remove duplicates. The
result (Λmax , S) depends only on V .
Remark 3.2 (the defect). The four ambiguity sources of Section 1 all preserve n, but equiv-
alence (1) does not: the encoding definition requires only some invariant rank-2 lattice, so the
same V admits a symbol over every finite-index sublattice Λ′ ⊂ Λmax (V ), with n · [Λmax : Λ′ ]
seeds. Concretely, the square tiling V = Z + Zi has the n=1 symbol of Example 7.1 and also an
n=4 symbol over 2Z + 2Zi; the two vertex sets are literally equal, hence equivalent under (1). A
baseline that canonicalizes the given lattice by HNF and minimizes over 24n frames assigns these
two symbols matrices of different shapes, so it is not canonical for (1); we verified this computa-
tionally on a faithful implementation. Any correct N must therefore recompute Λmax , as Stage A
does. Alternatively one may read lattice maximality as an unstated precondition on valid symbols;
then Stage A is input validation rather than a correction, and it certifies the precondition at the
same cost. Whether the convention of [1] already requires maximality should be checked before
attributing the defect beyond the problem statement we received; in an enumeration pipeline the
point is moot, since period-solving stages do not in general certify maximal periods, and a dedup
key without Stage A can leak duplicates either way.

Theorem 5.2 (soundness). N(M ) is a valid symbol and N(M ) ∼ M .
Proof. Fix a candidate CV (g, [o]). Its first two rows are a basis of gΛmax , a rank-2 lattice with
R-independent generators since g is an invertible R-linear map. Its seed rows are pairwise distinct
(distinct cosets have distinct representatives) and contain rep(0) = 0 (take s = o). The union of
the row cosets is g(V − o) = gV − go, so the candidate encodes a vertex set equivalent to V via
(g, c = −go) in (1). N(M ) is one of the candidates.

     Lemma 5.6 explains the behavior of the residual minimization in Stage E: whenever two frames
in Gmin × A are related by a genuine symmetry of the tiling, the candidates they produce are byte-
identical, so those ties never change the outcome (though each candidate is still built; the tie is
redundant, not free of cost). Distinct candidate matrices occur exactly at frame pairs not related
by a symmetry of V , i.e. when the star data is more symmetric than the tiling. On every uniform
tiling we tested, all frames in the restricted set were symmetry-related and exactly one distinct
candidate was ever built (Table 1).

    Three honest annotations. First, the baseline’s cost is O(24n · n log n) = O(n2 log n), since each
of its 24n frames also requires an O(n log n) build; our worst case matches it and our restricted
search improves the constant by the factor 24n/σa. Second, Stage A is not always quadratic: with
early exit it was measured to scale linearly on random maximal-lattice instances up to n = 4096;
but an adversarial family exists (a single deleted vertex class per cell of a large square grid, so that
many near-periods survive long verification prefixes) on which measured cost scales as Θ(n2 ), e.g.
6.7 s at n = 3599 against 0.22 s for all other stages combined. Filtering candidates to those whose
star matches the star of a rarest-star class (valid, since a true period preserves stars) reduces the
candidate count; it does not remove the quadratic family. Third, “σa = O(1)” is a hypothesis
about the input, not a theorem about tilings: every uniform tiling we ran has σ ∈ {4, 8, 12}
(Table 1), with the saving grace that a = 1 and all σ candidates coincided; and in a corpus of 400
random symbols, 131 required residual tie-breaking beyond such coincidences. The fair summary
of directness is: the frame search is provably cut from 24n to σa, the cut set is computed by a direct
rule, and its internal ties are provably harmless exactly when they come from true symmetries.

9    Limitations and open directions
T1 (directness). What is proved: the frame search is cut from 24n to the stabilizer σa of
the star data by a direct, minimization-free rule, and internal ties coming from true symmetries
are provably redundant (Lemma 5.6). What is not proved: that refined equivariant invariants
always pin a unique frame per symmetry orbit. The residual minimization therefore remains as
a correctness guarantee, and on symmetric tilings it demonstrably compares only copies of one
matrix. We do not claim σa = O(1) generically over any natural distribution of tilings; the
uniform tilings of Table 1 all have σ > 1, tracking their point groups.

T2 (entry bounds). As delivered, N has no intrinsic entry bound: rep reduces pivot columns
only, so free-column entries are bounded polynomially in the input encoding but not by tiling data.
A variant replaces the HNF basis by the Lagrange–Gauss reduced basis (unique up to at most 16
ordered sign/swap/tie choices, realized on hexagonal lattices; take the lexicographic minimum over
them) and reduces seeds into the fundamental parallelogram. Sketch of the resulting bound: each
ω k has coordinates in {−1, 0, 1}, so a vertex reachable from the origin by m unit-edge steps has
all four coordinates bounded by m in absolute value; reduced representatives lie within Euclidean
distance O(λ2 ) of the origin, and λ2 = O(n) since the cell area is Θ(n) and λ1 is bounded below by
the minimum vertex distance of unit-edge tilings. The missing step is the routine bounded-dilation
lemma converting Euclidean distance to path length in these tilings; we flag it rather than claim
                   √
it. Conversely O( n) is impossible: strip-stacked tilings in the style of the elongated triangular

forcing entries Ω(n).

T3 (hardness). Nothing is proved. We note only that once a frame (g, o) is fixed, the labeled
structure is rigid: a breadth-first traversal of the quotient graph with direction-sorted edges re-
constructs the symbol deterministically in O(n) operations, so no canonical-labeling hardness can
hide inside a frame. The entire search space of the problem is the 24n frame set, already cut
to the star stabilizer; a hardness reduction would need families of tilings whose equivariant local
invariants all coincide across inequivalent frames, which the rigidity of the fifteen vertex types
makes implausible, but that is an expectation, not a theorem.

Relation to prior sketches. Anchoring the origin at an extremal intrinsic label and fixing the
orientation by star minimization realizes, with proofs, the dual-graph canonical-labeling sketch
of [2, §5.10]; the contributions here are the equivariance framework making the restricted minimum
well defined, the coincidence lemma, and the lattice-maximality correction of Remark 3.2.
