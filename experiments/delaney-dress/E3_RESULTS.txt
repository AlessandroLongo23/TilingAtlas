5. METRIC DEVELOPMENT (exact Q(zeta_48))
====================================================================
  develop5.py walks the chamber system, places unit-edge regular polygons with
  EXACT Q(zeta_48) coordinates, rebuilds the neighbour tile-center at the
  neighbour's apothem on edge-crossings, and checks (a) COLLISION (two chambers
  forced to the same flag) and (b) per-tile-center m01 consistency.

  POSITIVE WITNESS: 4.8.8 develops to a 55-tile patch (18 squares + 37 octagons),
  ZERO conflicting tile-centers, edge-to-edge, all coordinates exact. The three
  regular tilings + all 93 k=1 candidates + all 59 k=2 candidates (<=8) develop
  with NO collision within the step cap.

  HONEST LIMITATION (measured, not assumed):
   - The collision/tile-conflict test catches FORCED combinatorial incompatibility
     (a true 3.4.4.6-corona would trip it -- but no such valid symbol exists to test).
   - It does NOT catch ANGLE defects: the mixed-sign ghost (270/540deg) and the
     hyperbolic symbol (K=-1/12) develop with NO collision (they spiral; exact
     points never coincide). Those are caught by per_component_flat / curvature.
   - 'Clean patch within cap' is a SEMI-decision, not a closure PROOF (the cover is
     infinite; a real tiling develops cleanly forever, but so does an undetected
     near-miss within a finite budget). A complete metric oracle needs periodicity
     detection (translation-lattice recovery), which I did NOT finish.

  So flat+regular does NOT trivially "always develop": development is a real,
  separate computation. For the cases tested, every axiom-valid + per_component_flat
  symbol developed without collision -- but that is EVIDENCE, not a proof that B2 is
  vacuous.

6. B2 VERDICT (is realizability a cheap finite check, or the closure problem?)
====================================================================
  For the REGULAR family at k=1, the data leans toward: the cheap combinatorial
  filters (DS axioms + regular labels + per_component_flat) are SURPRISINGLY strong.
   - 3.4.4.6 (the classic Chavey closure obstruction) is killed by the AXIOMS, not
     by metric closure -- it never forms a valid symbol.
   - No axiom-valid + flat symbol was found that DEMONSTRABLY fails metric closure
     (my development found 0 collisions among 93 k=1 + 59 k=2 candidates).
  BUT this is NOT a proof that B2 is free:
   - My development is a semi-decision (no periodicity proof), so I cannot certify
     that all 93/59 are truly realizable -- only that none was caught failing.
   - The 93 k=1 candidates vs 11 realizable tilings GAP (82 extra) is unexplained
     by collision: those 82 develop cleanly too. They are most plausibly LARGER-CELL
     (sub-symmetry) representations of the SAME 11 tilings -- meaning the real B2
     gate is "is this the MAXIMAL-symmetry quotient / does it correspond to a
     DISTINCT tiling", which is exactly a GLOBAL property (the symmetry group of the
     developed tiling), i.e. the same closure/periodicity computation the lattice
     method does.
  NET: B2 is NOT obviously a cheap finite check. The combinatorial filters remove
  MORE than expected (3.4.4.6), but distinguishing the 11 realizable tilings from
  the 82 extra abstract symbols requires developing the tiling and reading off its
  symmetry/periodicity -- the same hard global closure the torus method performs.
  B2 is the closure problem re-skinned, just with a smaller candidate set feeding it.
